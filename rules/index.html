Overview

Hollow Throne is a dark fantasy 1v1 card game. Two players, each commanding a unique hero with their own playstyle and signature power, build their forces and battle to destroy their opponent. Deploy creatures, cast spells, attach equipment, and invoke enchantments that twist the rules of the game in your favor.

Each hero has three core resources: Life Points (LP), their physical resilience; Sanity Points (SP), their mental fortitude and magical reserves; and Essence (E), the primary currency for playing cards — it resets each turn and must be generated, spent, or lost. Exhaust your opponent's body or break their mind — either path leads to victory.

Hollow Throne is a game of dwindling resources. Burned spells are gone forever. Creatures die and leave the cycle. Your deck shrinks over the course of a match, and when it runs out you must reshuffle your discard pile to keep drawing — but each reshuffle costs you LP or SP. The shared Marketplace offers reinforcements, but those cards cost Essence to buy and more Essence to play. This cost — paying LP and/or SP from your own reserves — is called Toll. Toll appears throughout the rules: cycle penalties, end-of-turn discards, emergency Essence generation, and more. Every point of Toll is split however you choose between LP and SP, so every payment is a decision about which resource you can afford to lose.

Each player brings:

  • 1 Hero card (sets your LP, SP, Essence income, and unique hero power)
  • A 15-card deck, chosen before the match from the hero's card pool (hero pools vary in size)
  • Access to the shared Marketplace — neutral cards available to both players

Win Conditions

You win when your opponent's LP reaches 0 — their forces crushed in battle — or their SP reaches 0 — their will and sanity broken. Win conditions are checked the instant either resource changes, mid-combat or mid-turn. The game ends immediately.

If both players reach 0 simultaneously, the player whose turn it is (the active player) loses.

Setup

  1. Choose heroes. Each player selects one of the available heroes.
  2. Build decks. From your hero's card pool, select 15 cards to form your deck for this match. Set the remaining cards aside — they are not used.
  3. Draft the marketplace. Each player secretly selects 3 neutral card types they want available. Reveal choices simultaneously and combine into a single list (duplicate selections count only once). Continue adding random card types until the marketplace contains exactly 10 distinct types — 2 copies of each — for 20 cards total.
  4. Set starting resources. Set your LP and SP trackers to your hero's starting values. Place your hero card face-up in your hero zone.
  5. Shuffle and draw. Shuffle your 15-card deck and draw 5 cards.
  6. Determine first player. Flip a coin or agree randomly.
  7. First player adjustment. The first player does not draw the mandatory +1 card during their first Draw Phase. They play their first turn with their initial 5-card hand.

There is no mulligan. Play with the hand dealt.

Card Types

Every card in your deck or the marketplace belongs to one of four types. Card type determines where it can be played, how long it stays in play, and what happens to it after use.

Hero

Each player has exactly one Hero card, placed face-up at the start of the game. Your Hero defines your starting LP, SP, Essence income per turn, and unique hero power. Your hero stays in play for the entire game — it cannot be destroyed, removed, or returned to hand. Heroes can be targeted by equipment and card effects, and can receive Bleed tokens and damage from combat or abilities.

Creatures

Permanent cards that occupy your battlefield. Each creature has three stats: ATK (damage dealt in combat), DEF (how much damage it can absorb before dying), and SV (the resource value you receive if you sacrifice it). Creatures form your primary offense and defense.

Summoning sickness: Creatures cannot attack the turn they enter play — they need time to get their bearings. They can block incoming attacks and use special abilities immediately.

Spells

One-time effects that resolve immediately when cast, then move to a zone based on their subtype:

  • Cycled: After use, the card goes to your discard pile and will eventually reshuffle back into your deck. Repeatable over the course of a long game.
  • Burned: After use, the card is placed in the Removed zone. Burned spells tend to be more powerful — but you only get one use, ever.

Spells are normally cast during your Main Phase. They can also be cast during Reactive Windows on your opponent's turn — but any spell played reactively is placed in the Removed zone, even if it would normally Cycle. The one exception: spells with the Reaction keyword keep their normal cycle/burn behavior when played reactively. See Reactive Play for the full rules.

Equipment

Permanent cards that attach to a creature or your hero, granting bonuses or activated abilities for as long as they remain in play. Only one equipment may be attached to each creature or hero at a time — attaching a new one immediately sends the old one to your discard pile. If the equipped creature leaves the battlefield for any reason, the equipment also goes to your discard.

Some equipment have abilities you can trigger by exhausting the equipment itself (rotating it sideways). Only the equipment becomes exhausted when this happens — the creature or hero equipping it is unaffected and remains ready.

Enchantments

Permanent cards that occupy your enchantment zone and reshape the game's rules while in play. Unlike creatures, enchantments don't fight — they alter ongoing conditions: boosting your income, punishing your opponent's actions, empowering your creatures each turn, or enabling powerful synergies across multiple turns.

Some enchantments have upkeep costs paid during your Upkeep Step. If you cannot or choose not to pay, the enchantment is destroyed and sent to your discard pile.

Zones

Each area of play has distinct rules for what enters, what can leave, and how cards there interact with effects. The board diagram above shows where each zone sits physically; this section covers the rules for each.

Turn Structure

Every turn follows the same six phases in order. You cannot skip or rearrange phases, though most phases are quick and automatic:

1. Ready Step

  • Ready all your exhausted permanents (creatures, equipment, and your hero card if exhausted).
  • Process Bleed: Each creature or hero carrying a bleed token takes 1 damage, then the token is removed.

2. Draw Phase

  • Replenish: Draw cards until you have 5 in hand.
  • Mandatory draw: Draw 1 additional card. You always draw at least 1 card, even if you started the phase with a full hand of 5.
  • You end this phase with up to 6 cards. The hand limit of 5 is enforced at the End Phase, not here.

If your deck is empty when you need to draw, reshuffle your discard pile into a new deck and take Toll(2) (cycle damage). If both your deck and discard are empty, take 1 LP draw damage per missing card instead — this bypasses the cycle entirely and cannot be split into SP.

3. Income Step

  • Reset Essence to 0, then gain your hero's base Essence per turn.
  • Add bonus Essence granted by any permanents currently in play (enchantments, equipment, or other card effects that increase your income).
  • Essence does not carry over — any unspent income is lost at the end of your turn.

4. Upkeep Step

  • Pay any upkeep costs on your equipment and enchantments. Each card that has an upkeep cost specifies what must be paid.
  • If you cannot or choose not to pay a card's upkeep, it is immediately destroyed and sent to your discard pile.

5. Main Phase

Take any number of actions in any order:

6. End Phase

  • Passive hero powers trigger now (end-of-turn effects on your hero card resolve).
  • Voluntary discard: You may discard any cards you no longer want. Each card discarded here costs Toll(1).
  • Hand limit: If you have more than 5 cards, discard down to 5. Each forced discard also costs Toll(1).
  • Feedback: Both players check for unspent non-income Essence. Each unspent point from non-income sources (sacrifice, discard-for-Essence, exhaust abilities, hero powers) costs Toll(1) per point to that player.
  • All temporary buffs expire.
  • All remaining Essence is lost.

Combat

Combat may occur once per turn, during your Main Phase. You choose when to initiate it, and you may take other actions before and after.

1. Declare Attackers

Choose any number of your ready creatures that are not affected by summoning sickness to attack. Each attacker becomes exhausted when declared — they commit to the attack regardless of what follows.

Attack triggers resolve now (keywords like Horrify, on-attack abilities, etc.). Your opponent then has a Reactive Window before blockers are assigned.

2. Declare Blockers

The defending player assigns ready creatures as blockers. Each blocker blocks exactly one attacker; multiple creatures cannot gang up on a single attacker. Any attacker left unblocked deals its damage directly to the defending hero's LP.

Restrictions:

  • Exhausted creatures cannot block.
  • Only creatures with Flying or Reach can block a creature with Flying.

3. Resolve Damage

All combat damage resolves simultaneously — there is no priority order within a single combat.

  • Blocked: Attacker and blocker each deal their ATK as damage to one another. Damage accumulates on each creature as counters against their DEF.
  • Unblocked: The attacker deals its ATK directly as LP damage to the defending hero.
  • Creatures at 0 or fewer DEF die immediately, moving to their owner's graveyard.
  • Vampiric creatures that deal damage heal their controller an equal amount of LP.

Reactive Play

The game doesn't pause during your opponent's turn — you can act. Either player may cast spells during two designated Reactive Windows on the active player's turn:

  • Attack Window: Opens after the active player declares attackers, before the defender assigns blockers. Use this window to remove a threat before it connects, buff your own blockers, or drain the attacker's resources.
  • Spell Window: Opens after the active player casts a spell and pays its costs, before that spell resolves. Use this window to counter the spell or respond to its setup.

In physical play, signal intent before the action resolves: the non-active player says "I respond" and declares their spell. If no response is declared, the action resolves without interruption.

Funding a Reactive Play

You have no Essence income on your opponent's turn, so resources must come from what you've prepared or what you're willing to sacrifice:

  • SP-cost spells: Spells with only an SP cost need no Essence at all. Your SP pool is always available.
  • Exhausting creatures: A ready creature with an Exhaust ability may use it during a Reactive Window. The creature becomes exhausted and cannot block this combat — weigh the trade-off carefully.
  • Sacrificing creatures: You may sacrifice a creature during a Reactive Window, choosing Offering (Essence) or Consume (SP) as normal.
  • Temporal Cache / Spell Vault tokens: Store tokens during your turns and spend them as Essence during any Reactive Window.
  • Toll for Essence: Toll(1) to gain 1 Essence — available at any time, including during Reactive Windows. A reliable last resort, but Toll-generated Essence is non-income and triggers Feedback if unspent at end of turn.

Rules for Reactive Spells

  • Any spell may be played during a Reactive Window, provided you can pay its cost.
  • One spell per window. You get one chance to respond. The reactive spell resolves immediately — it does not open another window. There are no chains, stacks, or priority passes.
  • Spells cast during a Reactive Window are placed in the Removed zone, even if they would normally Cycle. Exception: spells with the Reaction keyword keep their normal cycle/burn behavior when played reactively.
  • Counterspells cancel the triggering spell entirely — it does not resolve. The countered spell still moves to its appropriate zone (Removed if Burned, discard if Cycled). Both players lose the resources they spent.
  • Creatures cannot be played from hand during Reactive Windows — summoning requires the Main Phase. See Errata for card-specific exceptions (e.g., Spell Vault).
  • Enchantments and Equipment cannot be played during Reactive Windows.

Sacrifice

You may sacrifice any creature you control during your Main Phase or during a Reactive Window. Choose one:

  • Offering: Gain Essence equal to the creature's SV (Sacrifice Value).
  • Consume: Recover SP equal to the creature's SV.

Any triggered abilities on the sacrificed creature resolve first (e.g., "When this creature dies, deal 1 damage"). The creature then moves to your graveyard.

The Marketplace

The Marketplace is a shared row of neutral cards running down the center of the table, accessible to both players. It offers mercenaries, spells, and equipment that no hero deck contains — a common resource that both players compete to control and exploit.

Buying: During your Main Phase, pay a card's buy cost in Essence to acquire it. The card goes directly to your hand and can be played that same turn if you have the resources to do so.

Stock: The marketplace contains 2 copies of each card type. Once both copies of a card have been purchased, that card is no longer available. Act early if you want something before your opponent takes it.

Draft (setup): See Setup step 3. Each match uses a freshly drafted marketplace, so available cards vary game to game.

Hero Powers

Every hero carries a unique power that defines part of their playstyle. Powers come in two forms:

  • Passive — triggers automatically at its specified timing with no action required.
  • Exhaust — an activated ability you use once per turn during your Main Phase only. Exhaust your hero card when used; it readies at the start of your next turn.

Special Rules

Feedback

Essence is volatile — generating more than you spend has consequences. At the end of every turn, both players check for unspent non-income Essence. Each unspent point from non-income sources — sacrifice (Offering), discard-for-Essence, Toll-for-Essence, exhaust abilities, and hero powers — costs Toll(1) per point to that player.

Essence from your Income Step (hero base income plus any permanent bonuses) is exempt. You are never penalized for leaving income unspent.

Spending order: Income Essence is always spent first. If you hold 3 income Essence and 3 generated Essence and spend only 3 total, the income is consumed — the 3 generated Essence remains unspent and triggers Feedback. You cannot choose to spend generated Essence first to avoid the penalty.

Feedback applies to both players, not just the active player. If you generate Essence via Offering during a Reactive Window on your opponent's turn and leave it unspent, it triggers Feedback at the end of their turn. Every resource you actively generate must be used, or it costs you.

Unmade

Some card effects Unmake a creature rather than destroy it. An Unmade creature goes to the Removed zone permanently — not the Graveyard. The creature has not died: no death triggers fire, no sacrifice triggers fire, and no leave-play effects resolve. It cannot be returned by any recursion effect (Grave Touch, Mass Reanimate, Blood Bond, or anything similar). Only card text that explicitly says "Unmake" causes this. Normal destruction always sends creatures to the Graveyard.

Exception: Cards whose trigger text explicitly names Unmake — such as Bone Tithe ("dies or is Unmade") — do fire when a creature is Unmade, because the card itself opts in. This is not a death trigger. Only cards that say "Unmade" in their text acknowledge an Unmake event.

Damage on Creatures

Damage persists on creatures between turns. A creature with 3 DEF that takes 2 damage has 1 effective DEF remaining. It stays wounded until healed or it dies. There is no end-of-turn damage reset.

LP and SP Maximum

LP and SP cannot exceed their hero's starting values. Any healing or recovery that would push a resource above its starting value is simply lost — the excess is wasted. This applies to all sources: Vampiric combat damage, Consume, card effects, and hero powers.

Cycle Damage

Every time you reshuffle your discard pile into a new deck, take Toll(2). Burned spells and dead creatures thin your deck permanently, shortening the time between reshuffles — and the pain they bring.

Discard Pain

Every card discarded during the End Phase costs Toll(1), whether the discard is voluntary or forced by the hand limit. Discards caused by card or creature effects during other phases are free.

Draw Damage

If you must draw but have no deck and no discard pile to reshuffle, take 1 LP per missing card. This is direct LP damage — not a Toll, and not split between LP and SP.

Equipment

Only one equipment may be attached to each creature or hero at a time. Attaching a new equipment immediately sends the old one to your discard pile. When you exhaust an equipment to activate its ability, only the equipment becomes exhausted — the creature or hero it is attached to remains ready and unaffected.

Quick Reference

Turn order: Ready → Draw → Income → Upkeep → Main (with optional combat) → End

Essence: Resets to 0 each Income Step. Sources: hero base income, permanents in play, exhausting creatures, discarding cards (1 Essence each), sacrificing (Offering), hero powers, stored tokens (Temporal Cache / Spell Vault / Bone Tithe), or Toll(1) (pay 1 LP or SP to gain 1 Essence — any time). Unspent non-income Essence at end of turn: Toll(1) per point, both players (Feedback).

SP: Spent on spells, enchantments, and some hero powers. Does not recover naturally. Regain via Consume (sacrifice a creature for SP equal to its SV) or specific card effects.

Hand limit: 5 cards at end of turn. You draw to 5 + 1 bonus card each turn. Every End Phase discard — voluntary or forced: Toll(1) per card.

Reactive Windows: You may cast one spell in response to your opponent declaring attackers (Attack Window) or casting a spell (Spell Window). The reactive spell resolves immediately — no chains. Counterspells cancel the triggering spell entirely. Reactive spells are placed in the Removed zone unless they carry the Reaction keyword.

Unmade: Unmade creatures go to the Removed zone, not the Graveyard. No death triggers fire. Cannot be recursed by any effect.

LP/SP cap: Neither resource can exceed its hero's starting value. Excess healing or recovery is simply lost.

Victory: Your opponent's LP or SP reaches 0. Checked instantly whenever either resource changes.

Errata

Card-specific rulings that extend or clarify printed card text. The rules below apply only to the named card and override the general rules where they conflict.

Spell Vault

Spell Vault allows cards stored under it to be played at any time on any player's turn — broader than the two standard Reactive Windows.

  • A creature deployed from Spell Vault on your opponent's turn enters play before your turn begins. Summoning sickness clears at the start of your turn, so the creature is ready to attack immediately when your turn starts.
  • Spells deployed from Spell Vault on your opponent's turn are placed in the Removed zone unless they have the Reaction keyword.
  • Creatures, Enchantments, and Equipment deployed from Spell Vault enter play immediately regardless of whose turn it is.
  • You may place a card under Spell Vault and fire it in the same action if stored tokens already cover the cost.
